# Episode brief — "How Big Is the Earth?" (pilot)

_Drafted 2026-07-15 by the analyst; **operator must verify every fact marked [CHECK]
against the listed source before the script draft is approved.** This brief is the
authoritative outline; S2 may not invent beyond these notes._

**Spine:** a librarian in 240 BC measured the planet with a stick and a shadow — and
got within a few percent. It took the next two thousand years, several ruined lives,
and a war-torn decade of triangulation to do meaningfully better. This is the story
of the most patient measurement in history.

**Target:** ~35 min body + 2–3 min coda. Palette: warm antiquity → candlelit
instruments → cold expedition light → satellite black. Title candidates below.

## Chapter 1 — The Librarian and the Well (~6 min) [style: discovery]

- Cold open: Syene (modern Aswan), summer solstice, local noon — sunlight reaches the
  bottom of a deep well, columns cast no shadow. 800 km north in Alexandria, at the
  same moment, a gnomon (vertical stick) casts a shadow of ~7.2° — 1/50 of a circle.
  [CHECK: Cleomedes, *On the Circular Motions of the Celestial Bodies* I.7 — the only
  substantive surviving account of the method; 1/50 figure is Cleomedes' rounding]
- Eratosthenes of Cyrene: chief librarian of Alexandria, nicknamed "Beta" by rivals —
  second-best at everything. [CHECK: Suda/ancient biographical tradition — the "Beta"
  anecdote is well-attested but secondhand]
- The distance Syene–Alexandria: 5,000 stadia, measured by **bematists** — trained
  professional pacers who counted their steps. (NOT camels — the camel-caravan version
  is the pop retelling; bematists are better AND true.) [CHECK: Martianus Capella
  attributes royal bematists; classical scholarship on Ptolemaic surveying]
- Result: 250,000 stadia (Cleomedes; other ancient sources say 252,000). The stadion
  problem: which stadion? Depending on the length assumed (157.5–185 m), his error is
  somewhere between ~2% and ~16%. Be honest about this range on screen — the honesty
  IS the channel's brand. [CHECK: modern reviews of the stadion debate]
- Context beat: educated Greeks KNEW the Earth was round for centuries before —
  Aristotle's three proofs (ship hulls disappearing first, Earth's curved shadow on
  the Moon in eclipses, new stars appearing as you travel south). [CHECK: Aristotle,
  *De Caelo* II.14]
- Chapter close question: if we knew the size in 240 BC… why did Columbus expect
  Japan where the Caribbean is?

## Chapter 2 — The Error That Found America (~5 min) [style: discovery]

- Poseidonius re-measures a century later using the star Canopus and the
  Rhodes–Alexandria sea route; his method compounds two errors; later writers
  transmit a smaller Earth. Ptolemy adopts ~180,000 stadia in his *Geography* —
  a planet ~30% too small — AND stretches Asia eastward. [CHECK: Ptolemy *Geography*
  Book I; Strabo on Poseidonius]
- Interlude, so the story isn't Europe-only: Caliph al-Ma'mun (~830 AD) sends two
  teams into the Sinjar plain to walk a degree of latitude north and south —
  arguably the first state-funded scientific expedition; result within ~1% by some
  reconstructions. [CHECK: al-Biruni's account; modern reconstructions vary — present
  the uncertainty]
- Columbus 1492: he cherry-picks — Ptolemy's small Earth, Marinus' wide Asia, Alfraganus'
  mile misread as the shorter Roman mile — to make Japan ~4,400 km west of the Canaries.
  The Salamanca commission rejects the plan because they know the true size. Columbus
  wasn't the brave round-Earther of legend; he was wrong about the SIZE, and the experts
  were right. He never admits it — dies insisting he reached Asia. [CHECK: Columbus'
  marginalia in his copy of d'Ailly's *Imago Mundi*; Morison's biography for the
  Salamanca framing]
- Kill the flat-Earth myth explicitly in one line (the myth is 19th-century, largely
  Washington Irving's novelized biography). [CHECK: Irving 1828; Russell, *Inventing
  the Flat Earth*]
- Close: to do better than a stick and a shadow, you need a new idea — and it comes
  from a Dutch mathematician thinking about triangles.

## Chapter 3 — The Triangle Men (~5 min) [style: discovery]

- Willebrord Snellius, 1615: **triangulation** — measure ONE baseline with rods, then
  build a chain of triangles with angle measurements alone; each triangle inherits the
  baseline's precision. His Alphen–Bergen op Zoom chain, published as *Eratosthenes
  Batavus* (1617) — the title itself is the story: everyone knew whose problem this was.
  [CHECK: the 1617 publication and its degree estimate]
- Jean Picard, 1669–70: telescopic sights on quadrants; measures the Paris meridian
  degree at ~111 km — the first modern-accuracy value. [CHECK: *Mesure de la Terre*,
  1671]
- The Newton beat: tradition says Newton's Moon-gravity calculation finally "answered"
  when he plugged in Picard's new Earth size. Popular version is over-neat —
  present as "the story goes…" [CHECK: Westfall, *Never at Rest*, on the 1666 vs 1685
  chronology — flag the embellishment on screen; again, honesty as brand]
- The twist that sets up Act 2: Newton's theory predicts a spinning Earth must BULGE
  at the equator (oblate). The Cassini dynasty's own French surveys suggest the
  opposite — an Earth stretched at the poles (prolate). Paris vs London; a family
  business vs a new physics. The planet has a SHAPE problem now, not just a size
  problem. [CHECK: Cassini II's *De la grandeur et de la figure de la terre*, 1720]

## Chapter 4 — The Shape Wars (~7 min) [style: discovery → deep_time for landscapes]

- The French Academy settles it with the 18th century's version of a space program:
  two expeditions to measure one degree each at the extremes.
- **Lapland, 1736 — Maupertuis:** Tornio river valley, sleds, frozen instruments,
  16 months, comes home with a degree measurably LONGER than France's → Earth is
  oblate, Newton right. Voltaire's line — Maupertuis had "flattened the Earth and the
  Cassinis." [CHECK: Maupertuis, *La Figure de la Terre* 1738; the Voltaire quip's
  exact wording varies — verify or paraphrase]
- **Peru (modern Ecuador), 1735 — La Condamine, Godin, Bouguer:** planned as ~4 years,
  becomes a ~10-year catastrophe and the episode's emotional core: the expedition
  splinters; the surgeon Seniergues is killed by a mob at a bullfight in Cuenca;
  Godin stays in South America ~35 years; the naturalist Joseph de Jussieu loses his
  botanical collections and reportedly his health and mind; La Condamine finally
  rafts down the Amazon home. Their degree, measured at the equator under volcanoes,
  confirms the flattening. [CHECK each personal fate: Whitaker, *The Mapmaker's
  Wife*; La Condamine's own journal; Ferreiro, *Measure of the Earth* — the
  standard modern account]
- Beat: the irony that the "losers" (Peru team, decade of misery) produced the data,
  while Maupertuis got the glory in 16 months.
- Close: knowing the shape, France decides to USE the meridian — to define what a
  "metre" is. And sends two more men out, into a revolution.

## Chapter 5 — The Metre and the Secret (~6 min) [style: discovery]

- 1791: the metre defined as 1/10,000,000 of the quarter-meridian pole–equator through
  Paris. Delambre (north: Dunkirk–Rodez) and Méchain (south: Rodez–Barcelona) spend
  1792–1799 triangulating from church towers THROUGH the French Revolution — arrested
  as spies, towers torn down, war zones crossed. [CHECK: Alder, *The Measure of All
  Things* — the standard sourced account for this whole chapter]
- Méchain's tragedy: an inconsistency in his Barcelona latitude data (~3 arc-seconds)
  that he hides, reworks obsessively, and carries as a private torment until his death
  (yellow fever, 1804, back in Spain chasing the error). Delambre later finds the
  discrepancy in the notebooks and — understanding — smooths the record. [CHECK: Alder;
  keep Delambre's handling precise and fair]
- The punchline: the survey's meridian was slightly off (they couldn't know about
  local gravity/geoid effects), so the metre is ~0.2 mm "short" of its own definition —
  and humanity simply kept the bar. Every measurement you make today inherits a
  1790s survey error. [CHECK: Alder's figure]
- One-beat bridge: the technique scales — the Great Trigonometrical Survey of India
  (1802–) triangulates a subcontinent; a peak logged as "XV" is computed tallest and
  later named for a surveyor-general who never saw it: Everest. [CHECK: survey
  chronology; Everest's own objection to the naming]

## Chapter 6 — The Answer (~4 min) [style: cosmos]

- Today: satellite laser ranging, GPS, GRACE/GOCE gravity missions. The numbers:
  equatorial circumference 40,075 km; polar 40,008 km; flattening ~1/298.
  [CHECK: WGS-84 values]
- The geoid — the lumpy "potato Earth" gravity shape (GOCE visualization as image
  reference — but render as painterly art per channel style, not the ESA graphic).
- Scale anchors (fresh, not the billiard-ball cliché unless verified properly:
  the billiard-ball smoothness comparison is TRUE on tolerance but hackneyed — prefer:
  Everest-to-Mariana relief is ~20 km on a 12,742 km ball ≈ [compute fresh comparison
  in editorial pass]). [CHECK: do the arithmetic yourself; no borrowed anchors]
- Eratosthenes callback with the honest math: stick + shadow + pacers = within a few
  percent of WGS-84. Nothing beat it for ~1,900 years.

## CODA (~2–3 min, dark starfield) [tail]

- Register shift, reflective: we now measure the Earth from outside it, to
  millimetres — and watch it change: post-glacial rebound, the equatorial bulge
  breathing with ice melt. The question "how big is the Earth" turned out to have a
  moving answer. [CHECK: post-glacial rebound / GRACE mass-change findings, one clean
  verified example only]
- Last line territory: every map, every GPS fix, every metre stick traces back to a
  man looking down a well at noon.
- Tease next episode: size is only half the question. To know what the Earth IS made
  of, you must WEIGH it — enter a painfully shy millionaire, a torsion balance in a
  garden shed, and the experiment so delicate he had to read it through a telescope
  from another room. ("Weighing the World" — Cavendish 1798.)
